# Why Your CV Keeps Getting Rejected (And What Actually Fixes It)

You applied to forty internships last month. You heard back from two. One was an automated rejection. The other never replied.

Your grades are fine. Your certificates are real. You rewrote your CV three times. So what's actually going wrong?

Here's the answer recruiters won't say out loud: **your CV has claims, but no proof.** And in a pile of two hundred applications, claims don't survive the first ten seconds.

This isn't about your potential. It's about a very specific visibility problem and it has a very specific fix.


## The Gap Nobody Warns You About

Universities teach you to learn. Recruiters hire to reduce risk.

Those two goals are not the same thing, and the gap between them is exactly where most student CVs quietly die.

| What You Think You're Showing | What a Recruiter Is Looking For |
|---|---|
| I completed a Google certificate | Can they apply this skill to a real problem? |
| I know Python, React, and SQL | Is there evidence of that beyond a bullet point? |
| I'm a fast learner | Can I verify that before I stake my hiring decision on it? |
| I worked in a team project | Did they actually contribute, or just show up? |

This is not a character flaw. It's a structural mismatch. And once you understand it, fixing it becomes straightforward.



## What Happens to Your Application in Reality

Most CVs get roughly six to ten seconds of initial screening time. In that window, recruiters are not reading, they're scanning for **signals that reduce uncertainty**.

The question running in a recruiter's head is not "Is this person smart?" It is: "Can I verify what this person can actually do?"

<Callout type="warning" title="The Real Rejection Reason">
  Recruiters rarely reject candidates for lacking talent. They reject them for lacking verifiable evidence of that talent.
</Callout>

Two candidates. Same GPA. Same stack. Same role.

| Candidate A | Candidate B |
|---|---|
| Lists Python, React, SQL | Built and deployed a full-stack app using all three |
| Says "team player" | Has merged pull requests with review comments on GitHub |
| Completed an online course | Documented what they built and why |
| Claims to know Git | Has a readable commit history across real projects |
| Lists 14 skills | Shows 3 projects that prove 6 of those skills in context |

Candidate A isn't weaker. Candidate A just made belief harder. Recruiters will always shortlist the person who makes belief easier.


## The Five Things That Actually Get CVs Rejected

### 1. No Demonstrated Experience

This does not mean no paid job. For students, "experience" can be:

- a personal project you built from scratch and deployed
- a university team project where you had real ownership
- an open-source contribution, even a small one
- a freelance build, a hackathon project, a bug fix with a PR

If your CV says you know a technology but shows no work produced with it, there is nothing to evaluate. A recruiter cannot shortlist a guess.

### 2. A Skill List With No Context

This is the most common mistake on student CVs. A column of technologies listed in isolation is nearly useless as evidence.

Compare these two ways of presenting the same skill:

**Weak:**
> React · Node.js · PostgreSQL · Git · Docker

**Strong:**
> Built a full-stack task management app with React frontend, Node.js REST API, and PostgreSQL. Used Docker for local development and collaborated via Git with pull request reviews.

The skill is the same. The second version is ten times more credible because it has **context attached to it**.

### 3. No Portfolio or Public Work

A portfolio gives recruiters something they can actually inspect. Without it, your CV is a document of promises.

This doesn't have to be a fancy website. It can be:

- a GitHub profile with pinned, well-documented repos
- a simple personal site with project pages
- a written case study on a project you built
- technical notes or a blog showing how you think

The point is to give recruiters somewhere to go after reading your name.

### 4. No Signs of Collaboration

Technical ability is only half of what early-career hiring evaluates. Recruiters are also asking: **can this person work in a real team?**

If every project on your CV was solo and undocumented, they have no signal for that. The fix is simple; contribute to a shared repo, participate in code reviews, or join a team project where your work is visible and reviewable.

### 5. Tutorial Projects With No Original Decisions

There is nothing wrong with learning from tutorials. The problem is when your portfolio is *only* tutorial outputs: a weather app, a to-do list, a Netflix clone with no deployment, no real users, and no decisions of your own.

Recruiters are more interested in projects that show **tradeoff decisions, debugging, iteration, and real purpose**  than polished clones of something someone else designed for you to copy.

<Callout type="info" title="Small Scope, Real Decisions">
  A simple expense tracker you designed, deployed, and documented tells a recruiter more than a complex tutorial you followed step-by-step. Original decisions are evidence. Reproduction is not.
</Callout>


## The Proof-Beats-Claims Principle

Every claim on your CV has a proof equivalent that is ten times more persuasive.

| Claim | Proof Equivalent |
|---|---|
| "I know JavaScript" | Built an interactive dashboard with live API integration |
| "Experience with backend development" | Created REST endpoints, JWT auth, and database models for a real project |
| "I can work in teams" | Contributed via pull requests with commit history and review comments |
| "Passionate about coding" | Consistently shipped, iterated, and documented public projects |
| "Fast learner" | Picked up a new tool mid-project, documented the process |

This is not about dishonesty. Your original claims may all be true. The problem is that claims are cheap and proof is scarce, so proof always wins.

## A Practical Roadmap to Fix This

The solution is not another certificate. It is not another rewrite of the same CV. It is **building evidence that makes your existing skills visible and verifiable.**

Here is a focused sequence that works:

**Step 1: Pick one role direction.**
Frontend, backend, full-stack, data, mobile; one target. A focused profile is ten times easier to strengthen than a vague one.

**Step 2: Build 2–3 role-relevant projects.**
Make them practical and original. A frontend applicant should not have only console apps. A backend applicant should not have only landing pages. Each project should have a real purpose and at least one decision you made yourself.

**Step 3: Publish properly.**
Every project gets a GitHub repo. Every repo gets a README that explains what it does, what you used, how to run it, and what you'd improve. Deploy what you can. Screenshot what you can't.

**Step 4: Show collaboration somewhere.**
Contribute to an open-source project, even by fixing a typo or improving documentation. Join a team repo. Get one pull request merged by someone else. That is worth more than twenty solo projects to a recruiter assessing team fit.

**Step 5: Rewrite your CV around the evidence.**
Replace skill lists with project bullets. Every bullet should reference a real outcome, a real tool, and ideally a link. Remove phrases like "hardworking," "passionate," and "fast learner" unless the rest of the CV is already proving them.


## What to Fix on Your CV Today

Before you apply to another role, make these five changes:

- Move your **GitHub and portfolio links** to the top of your CV, not buried at the bottom
- Replace every **lone technology name** with a sentence showing how you used it
- Add **project bullets** with the problem, the tool, and the outcome, not just the name of what you built
- Remove any project that has no GitHub link, no deployment, and no real decisions behind it
- Cut filler phrases and replace them with **specific evidence** of the behavior they're claiming

## Conclusion

If your CV keeps getting rejected, the verdict is almost certainly not about your intelligence or your long-term potential.

It is a visibility problem. Recruiters are comparing hundreds of similar applicants, most of whom list the same technologies, claim the same skills, and provide no way to verify any of it.

The practical fix is not another application. It is building the kind of work that can be seen, inspected, and trusted, and then letting your CV point to it.

Stop asking your CV to do all the convincing on its own. Build proof. Make belief easy. That is what gets you shortlisted.
